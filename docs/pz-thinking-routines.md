# Project Zero Thinking Routines — Master Reference

Source: Project Zero, Harvard Graduate School of Education
Toolbox: https://pz.harvard.edu/thinking-routines
Full alphabetical index: https://pz.harvard.edu/thinking-routines-all

A thinking routine is a short set of questions or steps used to scaffold and deepen student thinking and make that thinking visible. All routines below are developed by Project Zero and are © President and Fellows of Harvard College, licensed CC BY-NC-ND. Each linked page includes the routine's purpose, application guidance, launch tips, and a downloadable PDF.

The one-line notes below are brief orientation summaries written for quick reference — always consult the linked page for the authoritative version of each routine.

---

## Core observation and inquiry routines

**See, Think, Wonder**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-think-wonder
Students observe closely, interpret what might be going on, and pose wonderings. A foundational routine for launching inquiry with objects, images, or artworks.

**See, Feel, Think, Wonder**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-feel-think-wonder
A variation of See, Think, Wonder that adds an emotional-response step.

**See, Think, Me, We**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-think-me-we
Observe and interpret, then connect the topic to yourself and to the wider world.

**See, Think, Make, Discuss**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-think-make-discuss
Extends close looking into a making/creating response followed by discussion.

**See Wonder Connect x2**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/see-wonder-connect-x2
A looped observation routine combining seeing, wondering, and connecting.

**Think, Puzzle, Explore**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/think-puzzle-explore
Activates prior knowledge (what do you think you know?), surfaces puzzles, and plans avenues for exploration at the start of a unit.

**Looking: Ten Times Two**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/looking-ten-times-two
Look closely and list ten words or phrases; then look again and list ten more. Builds sustained observation.

**Listening: Ten Times Two**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/listening-ten-times-two
The listening version of Ten Times Two, for music and sound.

**The Elaboration Game**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/elaboration-game
Learners take turns identifying and progressively elaborating on sections of an image or artwork.

**The Explanation Game**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/explanation-game
Name interesting features of an object and generate possible explanations for why it is that way.

**What Makes You Say That?**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/what-makes-you-say
The evidence-and-reasoning prompt: interpretations must be backed with visible evidence.

**Stop, Look, Listen**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/stop-look-listen
A careful-observation and claim-checking routine (see page for details).

**Colors, Shapes, Lines**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/colors-shapes-lines
Look closely at an artwork or image through its basic visual elements. Well suited to younger learners.

**Thinking with Images**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/thinking-images
A routine for using images as a vehicle for thinking (see page for details).

## Activating, connecting, and reflecting on learning

**3-2-1 Bridge**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/3-2-1-bridge
Capture 3 thoughts, 2 questions, and 1 metaphor before learning; repeat afterward and bridge the two sets to reveal how thinking shifted.

**Connect, Extend, Challenge**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/connect-extend-challenge
Connect new ideas to prior knowledge, note how they extend thinking, and identify what remains challenging.

**I Used to Think... Now I Think...**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/i-used-think-now-i-think
A reflection routine for articulating how and why thinking has changed.

**Think, Pair, Share**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/think-pair-share
Think individually, discuss with a partner, then share with the group.

**Chalk Talk**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/chalk-talk
A silent written conversation: learners respond on paper to a prompt and to each other's comments.

**Compass Points**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/compass-points
Explore a proposition from four directions before judging it: excitements (E), worries (W), needs (N), and stances or suggestions (S).

**Reflect, Connect, Project**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/reflect-connect-project
Reflect on an experience, connect it to what you know, and project forward to future implications.

**Creating Space for Learning**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/creating-space-for-learning
A routine focused on the conditions and environment that support learning (see page for details).

## Synthesizing and organizing ideas

**Headlines**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/headlines
Write a newspaper-style headline capturing the essence of a topic, discussion, or event.

**Color, Symbol, Image**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/color-symbol-image
Distill the core of an idea non-verbally by choosing a color, a symbol, and an image that represent it.

**Word-Phrase-Sentence**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/word-phrase-sentence
Capture the essence of a text with one word, one phrase, and one sentence that resonated.

**Generate-Sort-Connect-Elaborate**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/generate-sort-connect-elaborate
A concept-mapping routine: generate ideas, sort them, connect them, and elaborate.

**The 4 Cs**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/4-cs
A text-based discussion routine built around connections, challenges, key concepts, and changes.

**Take Note**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/take-note
A quick synthesis routine for capturing the most important, puzzling, or question-worthy points.

**+1 Routine**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/1-routine
A memory-and-consolidation routine where learners recall ideas and successively add to each other's notes.

**Peel the Fruit**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/peel-fruit
A "map" for deep understanding, moving from surface features toward the core of a topic.

**Beginning, Middle, End**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/beginning-middle-end
Use an image or object as the beginning, middle, or end of an imagined story to spark narrative thinking.

**Stories**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/stories
A routine built around the stories connected to a topic or object (see page for details).

## Questioning and possibility thinking

**Creative Questions**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/creative-questions
Brainstorm imaginative questions about a topic and select one to explore.

**Creative Question Starts**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/creative-question-starts
Practice generating rich questions using open-ended question stems.

**Creative Comparisons**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/creative-comparisons
Explore a topic by comparing it to something seemingly unrelated.

**Creative Hunt**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/creative-hunt
Hunt for creative and design features in objects or environments (see page for details).

**Imagine If...**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/imagine-if
Consider how an object or system could be made more effective, efficient, ethical, or beautiful — a maker-oriented redesign routine.

**Imagine If... (For Younger Children)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/imagine-if-younger-children
The early-years adaptation of Imagine If.

**What Can Be**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/what-can-be
A possibilities routine for envisioning change (see page for details).

**How Else and Why?**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/how-else-and-why
Generate alternative approaches or uses and reason about them (see page for details).

**Options Explosion**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/options-explosion
Rapidly brainstorm many possible options for responding to a situation or dilemma.

**Options Diamond**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/options-diamond
Map options for a dilemma across a diamond of trade-offs (see page for details).

**Does It Fit?**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/does-it-fit
A routine for weighing how well options or ideas fit a situation (see page for details).

**Portable Surprise**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/portable-surprise
A possibilities/curiosity routine (see page for details).

## Perspective taking and empathy

**Circle of Viewpoints**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/circle-of-viewpoints
Brainstorm the different perspectives around a topic, then speak from one of them.

**Step Inside**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/step-inside
Step into the perspective of a person or thing and speak from within it.

**Step In, Step Out, Step Back**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/step-step-out-step-back
Take another's perspective, acknowledge the limits of what you can know about it, then reflect on your own thinking.

**Think, Feel, Care**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/think-feel-care
Step into the roles of people within a system and consider what each thinks, feels, and cares about.

**Think, Feel, Care (For Younger Children)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/think-feel-care-younger-children
The early-years adaptation of Think, Feel, Care.

**Same, Different, Connect, Engage**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/same-different-connect-engage
Consider how you and another person are similar and different, then find ways to connect and engage.

**Same and Different**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/same-and-different
A simpler comparison routine, well suited to younger children.

**My Perspective, Other Perspectives (For Exploring Complexity)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/my-perspective-other-perspectives-exploring-complexity
Articulate your own perspective on something complex, then deliberately seek out others'.

**Who Am I?**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/who-am-i
An identity and perspective routine (see page for details).

**Seek to See**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/seek-see
A routine for looking beyond first impressions and assumptions (see page for details).

## Truth, evidence, and fairness

**Claim, Support, Question**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/claim-support-question
Make a claim, identify support for it, and raise a question the claim leaves open.

**Tug for Truth**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/tug-truth
A tug-of-war for evidence: pull together the reasons a claim might be true and might not be.

**Tug of War**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/tug-war
Explore the competing "pulls" on both sides of a dilemma before taking a position.

**True for Who?**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/true-who
Examine how the truth of a claim can look different from different vantage points.

**Facts or Fiction**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/facts-or-fiction
A routine for evaluating the reliability of information and claims.

**Red Light, Yellow Light**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/red-light-yellow-light
Identify "stop" and "caution" signals — features of a claim or source that warrant skepticism.

**Reporter's Notebook**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/reporters-notebook
Separate the facts and events of a situation from thoughts and feelings about it.

**Take a Stand**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/take-stand
Take and justify a position on an issue, remaining open to revising it.

**Sticking Points**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/sticking-points
Surface the points of tension or disagreement within a controversy (see page for details).

**Hotspots**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/hotspots
Identify the charged or high-interest moments within an issue or discussion (see page for details).

**Beauty and Truth**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/beauty-and-truth
Explore how works of art or texts convey beauty and truth, and where the two intersect.

## Objects, systems, and complexity (Agency by Design)

**Parts, Purposes, Complexities**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-purposes-complexities
Look closely at an object or system: what are its parts, what are their purposes, and what complexities does it hold?

**Parts, Purposes, Complexities (For Exploring Complexity)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-purposes-complexities-exploring-complexity
The complexity-focused variation.

**Parts, Purposes, Complexities (For Younger Children)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-purposes-complexities-younger-children
The early-years adaptation.

**Parts, People, Interactions**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-people-interactions
A systems-thinking routine: identify a system's parts, the people connected to it, and how they interact.

**Parts, People, Interactions (For Exploring Complexity)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-people-interactions-exploring-complexity
The complexity-focused variation.

**Parts, People, Interactions (For Younger Children)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-people-interactions-younger-children
The early-years adaptation.

**Parts, Perspectives, Me**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-perspectives-me
Examine an object or system's parts, the perspectives of those connected to it, and your own relationship to it.

**Parts, Perspectives, Me (For Exploring Complexity)**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/parts-perspectives-me-exploring-complexity
The complexity-focused variation.

**Name, Describe, Act**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/name-describe-act
An object-exploration routine for young children (see page for details).

**Ways Things Can Be Complex**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/ways-things-can-be-complex
Explore the different dimensions along which an object, system, or topic can be complex.

**The Complexity Scale**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/complexity-scale
Rate and discuss how complex something is, and what makes it so.

**Slow Complexity Capture**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/slow-complexity-capture
A slow-looking routine for gradually capturing the complexity of something.

**Layers**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/layers
Analyze a creative work through multiple layers of meaning and construction.

**Lenses**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/lenses
Examine a topic through a series of different lenses or angles (see page for details).

**Outside In**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/outside
A routine for considering outside influences on a system or topic (see page for details).

**Pass the Parcel**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/pass-parcel
A collaborative, turn-taking idea-building routine (see page for details).

## Global and civic thinking

**The 3 Whys**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/3-whys
Why might this topic matter to me? To the people around me? To the world?

**Circles of Action**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/circles-of-action
Consider possible actions in widening spheres — from close to home outward to the wider world.

**Unveiling Stories**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/unveiling-stories
Explore the layered stories of an image or event: the visible story, the untold story, the human story, the world story, the new story.

**Projecting Across Time**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/projecting-across-time
Imagine how a topic looked in the past and might look in the future.

**Projecting Across Distance**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/projecting-across-distance
Imagine how a topic plays out in other places around the world.

**Here Now / There Then**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/here-now-there-then
Compare present-day perspectives with those of another time and place.

**Values, Identities, Actions**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/values-identities-actions
Examine a work or issue for the values it evokes, the identities it speaks to, and the actions it invites.

**Think, Feel, Care** — see Perspective taking section above.

## Digital life and wellbeing

**Digital Habits Checkup**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/digital-habits-checkup
Reflect on and evaluate personal digital habits.

**Feelings and Options**
https://pz.harvard.edu/resources/feelings-and-options
Identify the feelings at play in a (digital) dilemma and consider possible ways to respond.
