# Physics (VCE study design)

Scope: how physicists model and explain light, energy, matter, motion, fields and electricity, from everyday phenomena through to relativity and quantum ideas.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — How are light and heat explained?:** students model the wave-like nature of light and the particle nature of thermal energy. Key knowledge: electromagnetic radiation and the spectrum, refraction and total internal reflection, dispersion, thermal energy transfer (conduction, convection, radiation), heating/state-change calculations, Wien's Law and blackbody radiation, links to climate change and global warming. Key skills: theoretical and practical investigation of wave behaviour, applying formulas to real contexts (optical fibres, mirages, rainbows).
- **Area of Study 2 — How is energy from the nucleus utilised?:** students explain and evaluate nuclear radiation, decay and nuclear energy. Key knowledge: nuclear stability and forces, radioactive decay and half-life, properties/effects of alpha, beta and gamma radiation, dose calculations, medical radioisotope therapy, fission and fusion, viability of nuclear energy for Australia.
- **Area of Study 3 — How can electricity be used to transfer energy?:** students investigate and apply a basic DC circuit model. Key knowledge: charge, current, potential difference, energy and power in circuits, series/parallel resistance, circuit analysis, transducers and common devices, household electrical safety (fuses, circuit breakers, RCDs, electric shock).
- **Unit 2, Area of Study 1 — How is motion understood?:** students describe and analyse the energy and motion of objects. Key knowledge: vectors and scalars, straight-line motion under constant/non-uniform acceleration, momentum, forces and Newton's laws applied to a case-study of motion.
- **Unit 2, Area of Study 2 — negotiated option:** students select one specialised option from a wide menu (e.g. climate science, flight, biomechanics, medical physics, astrophysics, sports science, particle physics) to investigate a contemporary issue or application using physics.
- **Unit 2, Area of Study 3 — student-designed investigation:** students plan and conduct a practical or research investigation generating primary data, drawing on knowledge and skills from Areas of Study 1 and/or 2.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — How do physicists explain motion in two dimensions?:** students apply Newton's laws to linear, circular and projectile motion. Key knowledge: forces in one and two dimensions, uniform circular motion (vehicles, satellites), projectile motion, impulse and momentum conservation, work, and energy transformations (kinetic, elastic and gravitational potential energy).
- **Area of Study 2 — How do things move without contact?:** students analyse gravitational, electric and magnetic fields. Key knowledge: field models and their directions/shapes, inverse square law, forces on charges and masses in uniform fields, motors, satellite orbits, and particle accelerators (including synchrotrons).
- **Area of Study 3 — How are fields used in electricity generation?:** students analyse and evaluate an electricity generation and distribution system. Key knowledge: magnetic flux and induced emf, AC/DC generation (alternators, generators, photovoltaic cells), transmission and transformers, minimising power loss over distance.
- **Area of Study 1 — How has understanding about the physical world changed?:** students analyse models of light and matter and use special relativity. Key knowledge: light as wave and particle (standing waves, diffraction, double-slit interference, photoelectric effect), matter as wave (de Broglie, electron diffraction), atomic emission/absorption spectra, Einstein's postulates, time dilation and length contraction, mass-energy equivalence.
- **Area of Study 2 — How is scientific inquiry used to investigate fields, motion or light?:** students design and conduct their own practical investigation with one continuous independent variable, presenting results as a scientific poster (aim, method, results, discussion, conclusion) supported by a logbook.

Cross-study skills: practical/laboratory work, logbook record-keeping, and quantitative problem-solving run through every unit; Unit 4's investigation is assessed via a structured scientific poster (max 600 words).
