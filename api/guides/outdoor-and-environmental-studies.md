# Outdoor and Environmental Studies (VCE study design)

Scope: combines practical outdoor experiences with theoretical study of human relationships, impacts and sustainability in Australian outdoor environments, drawing on Indigenous and non-Indigenous perspectives.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — Our place in outdoor environments:** students examine how humans connect with outdoor environments (custodianship, recreation, resource, spiritual, study site) and Indigenous/non-Indigenous ways of knowing Country. Key knowledge: biome features, motivations and responses to outdoor environments. Key skills: describe key terms and biomes, analyse motivations, compare Indigenous/non-Indigenous understandings, participate safely.
- **Area of Study 2 — Exploring outdoor environments:** students study how media portrayals, access factors (socioeconomic, cultural, age, gender, ability) and technology shape personal responses to outdoor experiences, including perceptions of risk. Key knowledge: influence of depictions/technologies, competence vs perceived vs real risk. Key skills: analyse depictions, explain access and technology factors, compare risk responses, interact sustainably.
- **Area of Study 3 — Safe and sustainable participation in outdoor experiences:** students plan, undertake and reflect on an outdoor experience, applying minimum-impact strategies and first aid. Key knowledge: route/tent-site planning, basic first aid (bleeds, CPR, immobilisation), risk management, equipment. Key skills: plan sustainable experiences, apply first aid, identify hazards and controls, evaluate strategies.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 2) — Understanding outdoor environments:** students study scientific (biotic/abiotic) and Indigenous land-management understandings of environments, plus vocational perspectives (tourism, guiding, agriculture, research/policy). Key knowledge: natural/human-induced change (fire, drought, climate change), vocational roles. Key skills: compare biotic/abiotic interrelationships, explain land management perspectives, compare vocational views.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 2) — Observing impacts on outdoor environments:** students evaluate human impacts (conservation, economic, recreational activities, urbanisation, technology) and community-based environmental action. Key knowledge: direct/indirect technology impacts, threatened species management. Key skills: evaluate impacts, compare technology effects, predict urbanisation impacts, evaluate management strategies.
- **Area of Study 3 (Unit 2) — Independent participation in outdoor environments:** students peer-lead an outdoor experience in a chosen environment, analysing user-group impacts and proposing management changes. Key knowledge: peer-led minimal-impact planning, weather adaptation, monitoring group impacts. Key skills: plan and lead safely, monitor and analyse impacts, propose sustainability improvements.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — Changing human relationships with outdoor environments:** students study Australian environments before human arrival, Indigenous relationships before/after colonisation, non-Indigenous relationships across historical periods, and one environmentalism campaign (Lake Pedder, Franklin River or Little Desert). Key knowledge: historical relationship changes, environmentalism's influence on policy. Key skills: analyse changing relationships across time, evaluate environmentalism's policy influence.
- **Area of Study 2 — Relationships with Australian environments in the past decade:** students examine Indigenous custodianship structures, conflicting values, and two conflicts over environment use (e.g. logging, feral species, water allocation), plus federal party environmental policy and social debates (climate change, renewables, water). Key knowledge: methods used to influence decisions, conflict-resolution processes. Key skills: compare relationships, evaluate influence methods and resolution processes, analyse policy differences and social debate influence.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 4) — The importance of healthy outdoor environments:** students study the pillars of sustainability, environmental health indicators (water/air/soil quality, biodiversity), threats (e.g. land degradation, climate change, fire) and mitigation strategies. Key knowledge: sustainability critiques, importance of healthy environments for wellbeing. Key skills: evaluate environmental health, analyse threats, justify environments' importance, analyse mitigation strategies.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 4) — The future of outdoor environments:** students study Indigenous and non-Indigenous land management, relevant Acts/conventions (e.g. Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act, EPBC Act, Ramsar Convention), and community/individual sustaining actions (Landcare, activism, sustainable consumerism). Key knowledge: effectiveness of legislation, community and individual action types. Key skills: analyse management strategies, evaluate Acts/conventions, propose improvements, evaluate community/individual actions.
- **Area of Study 3 (Unit 4) — Investigating outdoor environments:** students complete an independent investigation across at least two visited outdoor environments spanning Units 3-4, generating primary data (observation, interviews, images) plus secondary data, logged throughout. Key knowledge: drawing together relationship, health and sustainability understandings across at least four key knowledge points. Key skills: collect and evaluate primary/secondary data, document evidence in a logbook, evaluate selected environments.

Cross-study skills: an ongoing logbook of outdoor experiences underpins every unit as primary evidence, and every unit builds toward the same core moves, participate safely and sustainably, then analyse and evaluate relationships, impacts or management strategies, so practical fieldwork and analytical writing should be planned together across all four units.
