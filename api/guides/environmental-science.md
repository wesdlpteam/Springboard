# Environmental Science (VCE study design)

Scope: how Earth's interconnected systems (atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere) support life, and how pollution, biodiversity loss, development, climate change and energy use are managed sustainably, taught through content plus hands-on scientific investigation.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Unit 1, AoS1 — How are Earth's systems organised and connected?:** describe energy/nutrient movement across Earth's four systems (atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, lithosphere) and analyse how biotic/abiotic interactions sustain ecosystems. Key knowledge: food chains, webs and energy pyramids; systems thinking.
- **Unit 1, AoS2 — How do Earth's systems change over time?:** analyse how changes at various scales affect Earth's systems and assess stakeholder-driven management of a regional challenge. Key knowledge: transformative geological processes; data/modelling; the role of regulation and stakeholder values.
- **Unit 1, AoS3 — scientific investigation:** students adapt or design an investigation generating primary data on ecosystem components, monitoring or change.
- **Unit 2, AoS1 — How can we manage pollution to sustain Earth's systems?:** explain how pollutant characteristics affect Earth's systems and recommend management options. Key knowledge: pollutant dispersal, transport and toxicity (risk, dosage, bioaccumulation); regulation and stakeholder values.
- **Unit 2, AoS2 — How can we manage food and water security?:** compare agricultural systems, evaluate ecological footprint analysis, recommend food/water security options for a nominated region. Key knowledge: monoculture vs organic farming; footprint analysis and sustainability principles; water-use efficiency.
- **Unit 2, AoS3 — scientific investigation:** a student-directed investigation into how science addresses a selected pollutant or food/water security challenge.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Unit 3, AoS1 — Why is maintaining biodiversity worth a sustained effort?:** explain biodiversity's importance and change over time, analyse threats, evaluate management strategies for a selected threatened endemic species. Key knowledge: genetic/species/ecosystem biodiversity; ecosystem services; biodiversity measurement (Simpson's Index, conservation categories); threats (habitat loss, bioaccumulation, invasive species); legal frameworks (CITES, EPBC Act, FFG Act); sustainability principles.
- **Unit 3, AoS2 — When is development sustainable?:** explain sustainability principles, analyse stakeholder influence, evaluate an environmental management strategy via an in-depth case study (e.g. engineering, water, agriculture, land management). Key knowledge: dimensions of sustainability; circular economy thinking and cost-benefit analysis.
- **Unit 4, AoS1 — How can we respond to climate change?:** analyse factors affecting Earth's climate, explain measurement/modelling of variability, evaluate management options. Key knowledge: natural/anthropogenic drivers; albedo and greenhouse effect; carbon sequestration; adaptation and mitigation strategies.
- **Unit 4, AoS2 — What might be a more sustainable mix of energy sources?:** compare non-renewable (oil, coal, gas, nuclear) and renewable (biomass, solar, hydro, wind, tidal, geothermal) sources. Key knowledge: energy efficiency and thermodynamics; site rehabilitation; options for a sustainable energy future.
- **Unit 4, AoS3 — student-designed scientific investigation:** design and conduct an investigation (biodiversity, environmental management, climate change and/or energy use) generating primary data, presented as a scientific poster with a logbook.
- Assessed through School-assessed Coursework across Units 3 and 4 plus an end-of-year examination worth 50 per cent of the study score.

Cross-study skills: key science skills (investigation design, data analysis, scientific evidence, science communication) are assessed alongside key knowledge every unit; sustainability principles and stakeholder perspectives recur throughout, alongside fieldwork and practical technique.
