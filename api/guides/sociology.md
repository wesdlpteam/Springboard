# Sociology (VCE study design)

Scope: applies sociological theory and the sociological imagination to youth, family, deviance, crime, culture, ethnicity, community and social movements, drawing on primary and secondary research.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — Category and experience of youth:** students are introduced to sociological inquiry and the sociological imagination, examining youth as a social category and how its definition and media representation have changed over time. Key knowledge: nature of sociological inquiry, cultural/social/economic/technological factors shaping youth experience. Key skills: apply sociological concepts, analyse media representations, evaluate sources, synthesise evidence.
- **Area of Study 2 — The family:** students explore family as a social institution, its changing definitions and forms, and functionalist and feminist theoretical views, using comparative methodology across cultures. Key knowledge: social construct of family, contemporary family compositions, influence of government policy. Key skills: explain theoretical views, apply comparative perspectives, analyse influences on family life.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 2) — Deviance:** students study deviance through Durkheim's functionalist theory, Hirschi's social control theory and Becker's labelling theory, plus Cohen's theory of moral panic. Key knowledge: relativity of deviance, positive deviance, media's role in moral panic. Key skills: apply the three deviance theories, analyse moral panic's impact on those labelled deviant.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 2) — Crime:** students study types of crime, Australian crime-rate data, factors leading people to offend, and the rationale/effectiveness of punishment (retribution, deterrence, rehabilitation, restorative justice). Key knowledge: crime categories, international crime patterns, recidivism and alternative sentencing (e.g. Drug Court, Koori Court). Key skills: analyse crime data, compare national approaches, evaluate punishment effectiveness.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — Australian Indigenous cultures:** students examine material/non-material culture, the sociological imagination, historical suppression through government policy, reconciliation, and one contemporary issue affecting awareness of Australian Indigenous cultures. Key knowledge: ethnocentric vs culturally relativist representations, symbolic and practical reconciliation. Key skills: analyse representations and suppression's impact, evaluate awareness-related issues.
- **Area of Study 2 — Ethnicity:** students examine race, ethnicity, "othering" and Stuart Hall's theory of cultural hybridity, then study belonging and inclusion for one specific ethnic group in multicultural Australia using ethical research methodology. Key knowledge: Australia's ethnic diversity, factors shaping belonging (cultural practices, media, politics), ethical research principles. Key skills: explain hybridity theory, analyse belonging/inclusion factors, apply ethical methodology.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 4) — Community:** students study changing definitions of community via Tönnies and Maffesoli's theories, then analyse and evaluate one specific community's sense of belonging. Key knowledge: factors influencing belonging, effects of ICT/economic/social/political/geographic factors. Key skills: compare the two theories, analyse influences on a specific community, apply ethical methodology.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 4) — Social movements and social change:** students study forms of power, types and stages of social movements, and Erica Chenoweth's research on non-violent civil disobedience, then analyse one specific social movement in detail. Key knowledge: alternative/redemptive/reformative/revolutionary movement types, emergence-to-decline stages, power used by movements and their opposition. Key skills: analyse nature/purpose of movements, evaluate their influence on social change.

Cross-study skills: every outcome requires gathering and evaluating primary/secondary sources, applying sociological theory to real cases, and synthesising evidence into a conclusion, so research-and-evidence skills can be built consistently from Unit 1 through Unit 4.
