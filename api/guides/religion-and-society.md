# Religion and Society (VCE study design)

Scope: examines the nature, purpose and ethics of religion and how spiritualities/religious traditions interact with society, using the "aspects of religion" framework across multiple traditions.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — The nature and purpose of religion:** students examine the aspects of religion in general and apply them as a framework to selected spiritualities/traditions. Key knowledge: nature and purposes of religion over time, interrelated aspects of religion, religion's role in giving meaning to individuals/communities. Key skills: examine and apply the aspects of religion, interpret and synthesise primary/secondary sources.
- **Area of Study 2 — Religion through the ages:** students investigate how society and religion shape each other over time, including why traditions adapt, persist or fade. Key knowledge: socialisation of religious ideas across generations, effects of technology/philosophy/science on religion. Key skills: identify influencing factors, explain society-religion effects, discuss resilience/change/adaptation.
- **Area of Study 3 — Religion in Australia:** students study spiritualities/traditions in Australia past and present, shaped by colonisation, migration and secularisation, plus collective and personal identity and social infrastructure. Key knowledge: distribution/adherence data, government policy influence, interfaith and ecumenical interaction. Key skills: interpret adherence data, analyse composition trends, examine collective identity and social infrastructure roles.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 2) — Ethical decision-making and moral judgment:** students explore methods of ethical decision-making (ought ethics, virtue ethics, outcome ethics, etc.) and influences on practical moral judgment. Key knowledge: ethical concepts, decision-making methods and their theories, links between religion and ethics. Key skills: examine concepts, discuss methods, explain influences on moral judgment.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 2) — Religion and ethics:** students compare religious ethical perspectives across at least two traditions/denominations and explore the two-way influence between philosophy and religious ethics. Key knowledge: authorities/values/norms informing ethical perspectives, ethics-related aspects of religion. Key skills: discuss and analyse ethical perspectives, compare traditions.
- **Area of Study 3 (Unit 2) — Ethical issues in society:** students apply this knowledge to real public debates on two or more ethical issues, identifying contributors and the moral judgments used. Key knowledge: why issues are ethical, participants and their arguments, decision-making methods' strengths/weaknesses. Key skills: justify, examine debates, evaluate influence of contributions.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — Responding to the search for meaning:** students study the nature/purpose of religion generally, then the beliefs of one selected tradition (from Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Islam, Judaism, Sikhism) about ultimate reality, human purpose, suffering, death and relationships. Key knowledge: aspects of religion, categories of belief, connections between beliefs. Key skills: examine aspects and beliefs, analyse connections, use primary/secondary sources.
- **Area of Study 2 — Expressing meaning:** students examine how at least two beliefs from AoS1 are expressed through other aspects of religion (rituals, texts, community, ethics) to support meaning. Key knowledge: role of each aspect, relationships between aspects, how expressions support meaning. Key skills: analyse aspect roles, explain expression of belief, examine intended meaning-support.
- **Area of Study 3 — Significant life experiences, religious beliefs and faith:** students study one documented significant life experience of a member of the selected tradition and how it informed, reinforced or changed their faith and beliefs. Key knowledge: relationship between life experiences and belief generally, the member's adherence/understanding before, during and after the experience. Key skills: discuss the relationship, analyse the specific case, compare before/during/after.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 4) — Challenge and response:** students study three significant challenges (theology, ethics, continued existence) faced by the selected tradition and the stances/responses taken. Key knowledge: sources and significance of challenges, aspects of religion involved, stances taken and why. Key skills: discuss challenges generally, compare sources/significance, analyse and compare the three selected challenges.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 4) — Interaction of religion and society:** students study one significant challenge in depth, including its broader context, the tradition's stances/responses, and their influence on both the tradition and wider society. Key knowledge: interaction between religion and society, context/source/timing of the challenge, influence of responses. Key skills: analyse and examine the challenge, evaluate influence and outcomes.

Cross-study skills: nearly every outcome requires interpreting, synthesising and applying primary and secondary source material and reasoning through the "aspects of religion" framework, so lessons can build source-analysis and cross-tradition comparison skills consistently from Unit 1 through Unit 4.
