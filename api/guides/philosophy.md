# Philosophy (VCE study design)

Scope: teaches students to reason philosophically about existence, knowledge, value and belief, using primary texts and structured argument analysis.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — The nature and methods of philosophy:** students learn what distinguishes philosophical thinking from other kinds and practise the techniques philosophers use to build and test arguments. Key knowledge: argument terminology (premise, conclusion, validity, soundness, fallacy, etc.), the distinctive nature of philosophical thinking. Key skills: identify premises/conclusions, spot reasoning errors, apply reasoning techniques to evaluate viewpoints.
- **Area of Study 2 — Metaphysics:** students study at least two themes from materialism/idealism, nature of mind, personal identity, existence of God, free will and determinism, and time and space, using at least one primary text. Key knowledge: viewpoints, arguments and criticisms tied to the chosen themes, and links to contemporary debates (e.g. AI, punishment). Key skills: formulate philosophical questions, evaluate arguments, develop and defend positions.
- **Area of Study 3 — Epistemology:** students study "On knowledge" plus at least one other theme (a priori knowledge, science, objectivity), again grounded in a primary text. Key knowledge: nature of knowledge/belief/truth and links to debates like scientific authority or legal evidence. Key skills: same evaluative and argument-formation skills as AoS2, applied to knowledge questions.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — The good life and the individual:** using annually prescribed set texts, students explore pleasure, happiness, love, friendship, freedom and authenticity in relation to living well. Key knowledge: concepts (altruism, hedonism, virtue, etc.), viewpoints/arguments from set texts, objections and comparisons between them. Key skills: analyse and critique arguments, compare viewpoints, formulate and defend a philosophical position.
- **Area of Study 2 — The good life and others:** extends the good life to obligations, rights, justice and community, including relationships beyond one's immediate community and with non-human animals. Key knowledge: concepts (justice, rights, reciprocity, common good), set-text viewpoints and their interplay with contemporary living. Key skills: same critical comparison and position-defence skills applied to social/ethical questions.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 4) — Foundations of belief:** examines belief formation and justification, especially trust, testimony, expertise and disagreement, and the link between believing well and living well. Key knowledge: concepts (testimony, epistemic justice/injustice, peer disagreement), set-text arguments and objections. Key skills: analyse and criticise arguments, compare set-text viewpoints, reflect on believing well vs living well.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 4) — Contemporary applications:** students apply Area of Study 1 learning to real-world case studies containing epistemological issues. Key knowledge: relationship between the general questions and the case-study issues, objections arising from that interplay. Key skills: analyse and evaluate case studies using set-text concepts and arguments.

Cross-study skills: every area of study builds the same core moves (identify premises/conclusions, evaluate viewpoints against criticisms, formulate and defend a position in precise language) — lessons can reuse this argument-analysis scaffold across all four units, layering primary-text study and contemporary case studies on top of it.
