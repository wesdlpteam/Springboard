# Economics (VCE study design)

Scope: how consumers, businesses and government make economic decisions and allocate resources, from basic market models through to Australia's living standards and the government's tools for managing the macroeconomy.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — Thinking like an economist:** students describe the basic economic problem and roles of consumers, businesses and government. Key knowledge: microeconomics vs macroeconomics, scarcity and opportunity cost, the production possibility model, the three basic economic questions, economic systems, the circular flow model, traditional views of consumer/business/government behaviour and responses to incentives.
- **Area of Study 2 — Decision-making in markets:** students explain price and non-price factors in resource allocation. Key knowledge: demand and supply laws and curves, non-price factors, equilibrium price and quantity, market power (perfect competition to monopoly), business strategies to increase profit, one contemporary market case study.
- **Area of Study 3 — Behavioural economics:** students explain how behavioural economics complements traditional theory. Key knowledge: bounded rationality, bounded willpower, bounded self-interest, government and business strategies to influence consumer behaviour, at least one behavioural economics experiment.
- **Unit 2, Area of Study 1 — Economic activity:** students explain economic activity and growth and their effect on living standards. Key knowledge: material vs non-material living standards, the five-sector circular flow model, the business cycle, economic indicators, aggregate demand and supply, GDP as a growth measure, costs/benefits of growth, alternative living standards measures.
- **Unit 2, Area of Study 2 — Applied economic analysis of local, national and international issues:** students investigate two of four contemporary issues (changing labour market, international trade, distribution of income and wealth, environmental sustainability). Key knowledge: defining the issue, economic factors and importance, perspectives of economic agents, responses including government policy.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — Microeconomics: markets, resource allocation and government intervention:** students analyse how markets allocate resources and evaluate intervention. Key knowledge: relative scarcity and the PPF model, economic efficiency, demand and supply theory and elasticity, market failure (public goods, externalities, asymmetric information, common access resources), government intervention (taxation, subsidies, regulation), unintended consequences.
- **Area of Study 2 — Domestic macroeconomic goals:** students analyse factors affecting economic growth, full employment and inflation. Key knowledge: the five-sector circular flow model, the business cycle, aggregate demand/supply factors, measurement of growth (real GDP), employment/unemployment classifications, inflation measurement (CPI) and causes, consequences of not achieving each goal.
- **Area of Study 3 — Australia and the international economy:** students analyse the exchange rate, terms of trade and competitiveness. Key knowledge: gains from trade, the balance of payments, net foreign debt/equity, exchange rate and terms of trade determinants, international competitiveness, effects on domestic macroeconomic goals.
- **Area of Study 1 — Aggregate demand policies and domestic economic stability:** students discuss monetary and budgetary policy. Key knowledge: the RBA's role and the cash rate, transmission mechanism, monetary policy stance, government revenue/expenditure, the budget outcome, automatic and discretionary stabilisers, budgetary policy stance, strengths and weaknesses of each policy.
- **Area of Study 2 — Aggregate supply policies:** students discuss policies that improve supply-side conditions. Key knowledge: training/education, R&D, subsidies, infrastructure, tax reform, skilled immigration, trade liberalisation, market-based environmental policy, and their effects on productive capacity and living standards.

Cross-study skills: defining and applying economic concepts, constructing and interpreting economic models/diagrams, and gathering and analysing economic data to draw evidence-based conclusions run through every unit.
