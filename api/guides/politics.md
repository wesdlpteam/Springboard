# Politics (VCE study design)

Scope: examines how political actors use power to pursue interests and create stability or change, in Australia, globally and across the Indo-Pacific, using contemporary (last-10-years) case studies.

## Units 1-2 (Year 11)
- **Area of Study 1 — Power and national political actors:** students learn what gives political power legitimacy and analyse how Australian political actors (parliaments, courts, parties, media, movements) use power in one contested domestic issue. Key knowledge: power, legitimacy, authority, federalism, responsible government, separation of powers; sources/forms of power of Australian actors. Key skills: explain sources of legitimacy and power, identify actor interests and perspectives, construct an evidence-based argument about political significance.
- **Area of Study 2 — Power and global political actors:** extends the same lens to global actors (states, global governance bodies, non-state actors) responding to one global issue. Key knowledge: sovereignty, global interconnectedness, ideology, theories such as realism/liberalism/cosmopolitanism. Key skills: compare actor perspectives, explain causes/consequences of interconnectedness, evaluate political significance using sources.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 2) — Issues for Australia's democracy:** students investigate one issue (e.g. First Nations voice/treaty, constitution, media bias, integrity in government) and assess how well Australian democratic principles and institutions are upheld. Key knowledge: political culture, liberal democracy, strengths/weaknesses of Australian institutions. Key skills: explain causes and consequences, analyse how the issue affects democratic principles, build an evidence-based argument.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 2) — Global challenges to democracy:** students study one global challenge (e.g. authoritarianism, populism, big tech and media, sovereignty and national interest) and its impact on democracy's legitimacy and spread. Key knowledge: rules-based order, ideologies of governance, institutions of global governance. Key skills: explain how ideologies undermine/support democratic values, analyse causes/consequences, evaluate political significance.

## Units 3-4 (Year 12)
- **Area of Study 1 — Global issues, global responses:** students study one global issue in depth (climate change, global economic instability, development, or weapons of mass destruction), analysing its causes, global actor responses and the effectiveness of international law. Key knowledge: short-term (10-year) causes, global interconnectedness, effectiveness of institutions/laws. Key skills: analyse causes/consequences, compare actor perspectives, evaluate responses using sourced evidence.
- **Area of Study 2 — Contemporary crises: conflict, stability and change:** students study one humanitarian crisis (human rights, armed conflict, or mass movement of people), the actors' perspectives, and the extent to which responses achieve stability or change. Key knowledge: crisis causes, diplomacy/international law processes, challenges to resolution. Key skills: analyse causes/consequences, distinguish actor perspectives, evaluate significance and effectiveness of responses.
- **Area of Study 1 (Unit 4) — Power and the national interest:** students study one Indo-Pacific state (China, Japan, India, Indonesia or the USA), its sources of power and pursuit of national interests (security, prosperity, regional relationships/standing). Key knowledge: forms of power, foreign policy instruments, challenges to achieving interests. Key skills: analyse power and interests, evaluate effectiveness, argue the state's political significance from evidence.
- **Area of Study 2 (Unit 4) — Australia in the Indo-Pacific:** students examine Australia's national interests and relations with three regional states (one Pacific Islands Forum state plus two others), evaluating cooperation versus conflict. Key knowledge: Australia's forms of power and foreign policy instruments, perspectives on its regional role. Key skills: analyse perspectives, evaluate degree of cooperation/conflict achieved.

Cross-study skills: every area of study repeats the same inquiry cycle, ask political questions, interpret sources for actor interests/perspectives, explain causes and consequences, and construct an evidence-based argument about political significance and stability/change, so lessons can reuse that scaffold across all four units.
