How Springboard works

Turn any stimulus (an image, a short video, or an article) into a ready-to-run, five-minute lesson launch for any subject and year level, exported as an editable Wesley PowerPoint.

← Back to Springboard

The idea

Springboard removes the prep friction from starting a lesson well. You bring a spark (a picture, a clip, a news article), and in about two minutes you walk away with four editable slides that get students thinking hard from the very first minute. It works across the whole school, from Prep to VCE, and every deck comes out in the Wesley brand.

The four slides

Slide 1

Ignite

A bold provocation built from your stimulus (a big question and the image itself) to hook attention in the first minute.

Slide 2

Think

A real Harvard thinking routine, with clear steps, to scaffold how students reason about the stimulus.

Slide 3

Launch

The bridge into today's actual lesson and its curriculum focus, so the thinking connects to what you're teaching.

Slide 4

Reflect

A short end-of-lesson revisit that also nudges students to think about how they learned, and always ends with a look forward to next time (optional).

Every field is editable in the live preview, any single slide can be regenerated on its own, and teacher notes (facilitation, timing, differentiation, curriculum links, extensions) travel in the PowerPoint's Notes pane.

The flow

  1. Set the class. Choose the curriculum, subject and year level. (For VCE Year 11 or 12, the Subject box becomes a dropdown of the 50 VCE studies, see below.)
  2. Add a stimulus. Drop in one or more images or short videos, or paste / upload an article (or a link, which becomes a QR code).
  3. Analyse. The tool reads the stimulus and suggests a thinking routine that fits it and the age group. Pick one (or browse all of them).
  4. Generate. You get the four slides. Edit any wording, regenerate a single slide, or add a gentle content-advisory slide if a theme is sensitive.
  5. Download. Save the editable Wesley PowerPoint and drop it straight into your lesson.

The thinking beneath

Springboard doesn't just paraphrase your stimulus. Behind the scenes it writes every lesson against a small set of well-evidenced teaching ideas, so the output is designed to teach, not just to fill slides.

Made to Stick (Chip & Dan Heath)

Every deck is written to make ideas stick, using the book's six principles (SUCCESs), and to beat what the authors call the Curse of Knowledge, the trap of forgetting what it feels like not to already understand something. This runs automatically. There's no switch for you to set.

  • Simple: find the one core idea and lead with it in a compact, memorable line, this is prioritising, not dumbing down.
  • Unexpected: open a real curiosity gap students need to close (get them to commit to a guess first), never a gimmick.
  • Concrete: specific people, places and sensory detail they can picture, not abstract jargon.
  • Credible: let students test the claim themselves ("see for yourself"), or back it with a vivid detail or a human-scale number, not a bare statistic.
  • Emotional: a reason to care, focused on one person or one real stake, and tied to who students are.
  • Story: the launch is shaped as a small story (a challenge, a connection, or a creative breakthrough) so students feel moved to act.

These six run right through the deck, from the Ignite hook to the Reflect close.

Harvard Project Zero Thinking Routines

The "Think" slide is scaffolded by a genuine Visible Thinking routine from Harvard's Project Zero toolbox, the same routines used to make student thinking visible in classrooms worldwide. All of them are built in, and the tool tailors the routine's real moves to your specific stimulus rather than inventing its own.

Metacognition in the Reflect (thinking about thinking)

The "Reflect" slide doesn't only revisit what students think, it prompts them to reflect on how they learned, grounded in the research on metacognition (the plan–monitor–evaluate model used in the EEF's guidance). Every lesson closes with at least one forward-looking prompt (what I'd do differently, or which strategy I'll reuse, next time), tied to the actual lesson rather than a generic "how did it go".

  • Task: did my plan or approach work today, and how do I know?
  • Strategy: which strategy helped most, and what would help me use it better?
  • Self: what did I learn about myself as a learner?

The teacher notes carry the full question bank and a "least help first" way to run it, so the reflection feeds straight into planning the next lesson.

VCE study designs (Year 11 & 12)

When you set the class to VCE and Year 11 or 12 and choose your study, Springboard quietly loads a condensed version of that subject's official VCAA study design and feeds it to the lesson writer, so the "Launch" slide connects to the real Areas of Study and outcomes (Units 1–2 for Year 11, Units 3–4 for Year 12).

Pitched to the year level

The language and depth adapt to the band: concrete and inquiry-led for younger students, analytical and real-world for the middle years, and critical, ethical and conceptual for senior students.

Behind the scenes

Lessons are written by an AI model. The key that runs it lives on a secure Wesley backend, not in the app and not on your device, so you never enter a key, and nothing is stored on your computer. Your stimulus text or image is sent to the model only to generate the lesson.

✎

You are always in charge. AI-generated content should be read before class. Every slide is editable, sensitive themes can trigger an optional heads-up slide for students, and nothing is final until you download it.
